Water Conflict and Refugee Flows in the Arab World

For CAPP 30239 Data Visualization

Sam Pavlekovsky

Water is essential to life. It is for that reason that it is often one of the first things to suffer during conflict. In political strife or full-on war, purposefully weaponized or accidentally destroyed, lack of access to this resource places immense stress on victims of conflict. In turn, water can be a major factor among the causes of displaced populations and refugees.

In the Arab World, particularly the Middle East and North Africa region (MENA), many countries are predominantly arid or desert. Water is naturally a stressed resource, requiring careful management, extensive infrastructure, or diplomacy. For example, Israel has constructed extensive transport canals and irrigation systems that allow it to use water in trade with neighboring Jordan.[1] The situation has been exacerbated by the continuing effects of climate change and by the civil wars and unrest which have spread throughout the region since the Arab Spring in 2011.

This page explores data related to MENA refugees and water conflicts between 2010 and 2022. It will seek to elucidate if there is an observable relationship between water-related conflicts and refugee numbers, or if water issues only exert an indirect effect.

The refugee crisis in the Middle East

This crisis is well known, especially from the highly publicized controversies over European migration laws and policies during its peak. Although unrest had been fomenting in some Arab countries since the mid-2000s or long before, I am focusing on the most recent decade. During this period, the world saw multiple revolutions overthrow dictatorial regimes, although only Tunisia has approached success in maintaining the changes. The aftershocks of the Arab Spring led to civil wars in Libya, Yemen, and Syria, with two of the three ongoing. Lastly, it was during this decade that ISIS/ISIL strove for power, creating more conflict and tying into the Syrian civil war.

How does water affect refugees?

The Pacific Institute's Water Conflict Chronology classifies conflicts according to the role water plays: a casualty, trigger, or weapon. Casualties are by far the most common. Since water resource and infrastructure are central to population centers, they are at high risk for damage from missiles/bombing and other conflicts. These casualties can take a number of forms: it may lead to complete disruption of water access, with dire implications for agriculture and hydration. However, it can also lead to contamination of water supplies. Among the effects of the Yemeni civil war was a severe cholera epidemic caused by a lack of safe water sources.[2]

Instances where water is a trigger are generally protests or riots related to current drought conditions and water shortages, or over planned/threatened service cuts. In some cases this escalates into further conflict, including armed conflict. Weaponization of water often corresponds to a battle over strategic water infrastructure points, like a source or a dam. It can also include deliberately cutting off or destroying water access, such as in besieged cities. One drastic example of water as a weapon was the draining of the Mesopotamian marshes in Iraq by Saddam Hussein, with the intention of driving out and destroying the way of life for the traditional inhabitants of that area.[3]

While most water conflicts will not produce refugees as intentionally as this environmental disaster in the Iraqi marshes, they bring instability to daily life. The risk of failing farms, disease, or simply running out of drinking water can strongly motivate - or force - people to migrate.[4] Over the past decade, we can see that countries with the most water conflicts correspond to some of the countries with the highest numbers of refugees. The civil wars in Yemen, Libya, and Syria, as well as the war against ISIS/ISIL in Syria and Iraq, show up in high numbers of water casualties. Palestine also suffered many water-related casualties. Although conflict in the past decade has predominantly occurred in the Gaza Strip, such as the 2014 Gaza war, the majority of water events reported were in the West Bank and involved Palestinians and Israeli settlers clashing over expansion. These events included destruction of individual buildings and orchards. In some cases, the Israeli military was also involved.

Conclusions

The scatterplot to the right graphs number of refugees from each country over the 12-year timespan against the number of water-related conflicts. Despite the correlation of countries with many conflicts and many refugees observed above, there does not appear to be a strong relationship between the two datasets. In particular, for the five countries with the most conflicts (which are also most easily visible on this graph), Syria does not show any apparent linear trend, while Iraq, Yemen, Palestine, and Libya demonstrate shallow slopes. Further exploring each country with the visualization below, we can observe the refugee trends corresponding with revolutions and war. However, although in some instances the water conflicts and spikes in refugee numbers line up, it is neither consistent nor dramatic enough to suggest a direct effect of water conflicts on refugee movements.

Thus I conclude that there is not a direct relationship between refugees and water conflicts. Instead, based on the datasets available we can only characterize both as effects of the underlying political instability and warfare in the MENA region.

Stepping briefly into personal reflections on this result, while I did not expect either to find or not find a relationship, I am disappointed that there was not one. Climate-related migration and refugeeism is a relatively unrecognized and unprotected category of migration, yet it is expected to continue rising due to climate change. From sea level rise displacing coastal populations, to droughts and heat waves preventing agriculture, to increasing salinity of groundwater, the potential environmental motivations for refugees keep becoming more severe. In the face of slow adaptation and mitigation measures, the least governments can do to support these populations is give them the protection they deserve and recognize the reasons for their movement.
